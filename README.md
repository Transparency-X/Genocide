## 1. Executive Summary

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of genocide as defined under international law, examining its historical manifestations alongside emerging technology-enabled forms that pose novel challenges to prevention, detection, and prosecution. The report integrates the legal framework established by the 1948 UN Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide with contemporary developments in artificial intelligence, surveillance technology, autonomous weapons, and digital platforms.

Key findings include:

- The five specific acts defined in Article II of the Genocide Convention remain legally valid but are being executed through technologically mediated means that complicate intent attribution and evidentiary standards.
- Social media and encrypted communication platforms have replicated and exceeded the incitement role historically played by RTLM in Rwanda, while diffusing accountability across algorithmic systems and corporate entities.
- Smart city infrastructure, AI-driven predictive systems, and biometric surveillance grids enable ambient, invisible forms of group destruction that evade traditional humanitarian monitoring.
- The same technologies enabling modern atrocities (satellite imagery, digital communications metadata, IoT sensor data) create unprecedented forensic evidence trails for future prosecution.
- Ireland and the UK possess legal frameworks for genocide prosecution through universal jurisdiction, though these remain underutilized.

## 2. The Legal Definition of Genocide

### 2.1 The 1948 UN Convention

The internationally recognized definition of genocide was established by the **United Nations Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide**, adopted by the UN General Assembly on 9 December 1948 and entering into force on 12 January 1951. As of 2026, 153 states are parties to the Convention.

### 2.2 Article II: The Specific Acts

According to Article II of the Convention, genocide is defined as any of the following acts committed with the **"intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group"**:

1. **Killing members of the group**
2. **Causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group**
3. **Deliberately inflicting on the group conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part**
4. **Imposing measures intended to prevent births within the group**
5. **Forcibly transferring children of the group to another group**

### 2.3 The Critical Threshold: Intent to Destroy

Unlike crimes against humanity, war crimes, or mass killings, the legal threshold for genocide specifically requires proving the **"intent to destroy"** a protected group. This dolus specialis (special intent) distinguishes genocide from other atrocities and has been the primary obstacle to successful prosecution in several cases.

The International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY) established in *Prosecutor v. Krstić* (2001) that intent can be inferred from:
- The general context of the perpetration of other culpable acts systematically directed against the same group
- The scale of atrocities committed
- The systematic targeting of victims on account of their membership in a particular group
- The repetition of destructive and discriminatory acts
- The existence of a genocidal plan or policy

### 2.4 Protected Groups

The Convention limits protected groups to four categories: **national, ethnical, racial, or religious**. Notably excluded are political groups, social groups, and gender-based groups—a limitation that has generated significant scholarly and legal debate. The International Criminal Court's *Elements of Crimes* (2002) has expanded interpretive scope, but the core four categories remain.

### 2.5 Technology and Intent Attribution

The emergence of AI-driven systems poses novel challenges to intent attribution:

| Challenge | Description | Legal Implication |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Algorithmic opacity | Machine learning systems operate as "black boxes" | Difficulty proving human intent behind discriminatory outcomes |
| Distributed agency | Decisions emerge from human-algorithm interactions | Unclear whether intent resides with programmer, operator, or system |
| Automated scale | AI enables mass harm without direct human action per instance | Traditional mens rea frameworks may not accommodate autonomous harm |
| Platform diffusion | Social media algorithms amplify content without explicit human curation | Accountability gaps between individual posts and systemic incitement |

## 3. Notable Historical and Modern Genocides

### 3.1 20th Century Genocides

#### The Armenian Genocide (1915–1923)
The systematic deportation and mass murder of approximately 1.5 million Armenians by the Ottoman Empire during and after World War I. Methods included death marches into the Syrian desert, mass drownings, and concentration camps. The term "genocide" itself was coined by Raphael Lemkin in 1944 specifically to describe the Armenian and Jewish atrocities.

**Legal Status:** Recognized by 31 countries and the European Parliament. Turkey denies genocide classification. No international tribunal was established.

#### The Holocaust (1941–1945)
The state-sponsored, systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of others (Roma, disabled persons, political prisoners, LGBTQ+ individuals, Soviet POWs) by Nazi Germany and its collaborators. Methods evolved from mass shootings (Einsatzgruppen) to industrialized gas chambers (Auschwitz-Birkenau, Treblinka, Sobibór).

**Legal Status:** Prosecuted at the International Military Tribunal (Nuremberg, 1945–46) and subsequent trials. Established the foundational jurisprudence for crimes against humanity and genocide.

#### The Cambodian Genocide (1975–1979)
The killing of approximately 1.7 to 2.2 million Cambodians (roughly 25% of the population) under the Khmer Rouge regime led by Pol Pot. Victims included the previous government's leadership, middle-class professionals, intellectuals, and ethnic minorities (Cham Muslims, Vietnamese Cambodians, Chinese Cambodians). Death resulted from executions, forced labor, starvation, and disease in an attempt to create an agrarian socialist society.

**Legal Status:** Prosecuted by the Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia (ECCC), established 2006. Convictions secured against Nuon Chea and Khieu Samphan.

#### The Rwandan Genocide (1994)
The mass slaughter of an estimated 500,000 to 1,000,000 ethnic Tutsis, Twa, and moderate Hutus over approximately 100 days (7 April – 15 July 1994). The violence was carried out by Hutu-majority militias (Interahamwe) and government forces following decades of ethnocentric tensions exacerbated by Belgian colonial rule.

**Legal Status:** Prosecuted by the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR, 1994–2015). Landmark conviction of RTLM media executives for incitement to genocide.

#### The Bosnian Genocide (1992–1995)
Occurring during the breakup of Yugoslavia, this included ethnic cleansing and targeted massacres of Bosniak (Bosnian Muslim) civilians. The Srebrenica massacre of July 1995, where 8,372 Bosniak men and boys were killed, was specifically ruled as genocide by the ICTY and the International Court of Justice.

**Legal Status:** Prosecuted by the ICTY (1993–2017). Convictions of Radovan Karadžić and Ratko Mladić.

### 3.2 21st Century Atrocity Crimes

#### Darfur, Sudan (2003–Present)
The Sudanese government and Janjaweed militia targeted non-Arab ethnic groups (Fur, Masalit, Zaghawa) in Darfur. Estimated 300,000 killed, 2.7 million displaced. The ICC issued arrest warrants for Omar al-Bashir (2009, 2010) for genocide, crimes against humanity, and war crimes—the first sitting head of state indicted by the ICC.

**Legal Status:** ICC indictments outstanding; al-Bashir was arrested in 2019 but not transferred to The Hague.

#### The Rohingya Crisis, Myanmar (2017–Present)
The Myanmar military (Tatmadaw) launched a "clearance operation" against the Rohingya Muslim minority in Rakhine State. Over 10,000 killed, 740,000+ fled to Bangladesh. The Gambia brought a case to the ICJ alleging violations of the Genocide Convention (2019). The ICC authorized investigation into crimes against humanity (2019).

**Legal Status:** ICJ provisional measures ordered (2020); ICC investigation ongoing. Myanmar not party to ICC Rome Statute; jurisdiction based on Bangladesh's membership (cross-border crimes).

#### Yazidi Genocide, Iraq (2014–2017)
ISIS targeted the Yazidi religious minority in Sinjar, Iraq. Thousands killed, approximately 7,000 women and children enslaved. The UN Independent International Commission of Inquiry on Syria confirmed genocide (2016). German courts have prosecuted ISIS members under universal jurisdiction.

**Legal Status:** German courts have convicted multiple ISIS members (e.g., Taha al-J., Jennifer W.) for crimes against humanity and genocide-related charges.

#### Tigray Conflict, Ethiopia (2020–2022)
The Ethiopian government and allied forces engaged in widespread atrocities against the Tigrayan ethnic group in the Tigray Region. Estimated 600,000 killed from violence and famine. The Ethiopian Human Rights Commission and OHCHR joint investigation documented widespread violations.

**Legal Status:** No international tribunal established. ICC has not opened investigation. AU-led peace process (Pretoria Agreement, 2022) included transitional justice components.

#### Gaza (2023–Present)
The ongoing conflict between Israel and Hamas has generated allegations of genocide from multiple states and international organizations. South Africa brought a case to the ICJ alleging violations of the Genocide Convention (December 2023). The ICJ issued provisional measures (January 2024, March 2024, May 2024) ordering Israel to prevent genocidal acts and ensure humanitarian access.

**Legal Status:** ICJ proceedings ongoing. ICC Prosecutor sought arrest warrants (May 2024) for Israeli Prime Minister, Defense Minister, and Hamas leaders.

## 4. Specific Acts of Genocide: Historical vs. Technology-Enabled Manifestations

The following table maps each specific act defined in Article II against historical examples and their modern technology-enabled equivalents. This framework is essential for updating legal definitions, forensic methodologies, and prevention architectures.

| **(a) Killing members of the group** | **Holocaust:** Gas chambers, mass shootings (Einsatzgruppen), death marches; **Armenian Genocide:** Death marches into Syrian desert, mass drownings; **Rwandan Genocide:** Machete massacres, roadblock killings; **Bosnian Genocide:** Srebrenica massacre of 8,372+ Bosniak men and boys | **AI-targeted drone strikes:** Myanmar military's documented use of AI-enhanced satellite imagery for Rohingya neighborhood targeting; **Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS):** Autonomous drones with facial/ethnic recognition capability (e.g., Kargu-2 in Libya, 2020); **Deepfake assassination orders:** Synthetic media used to impersonate commanders ordering killings via encrypted channels; **Social media-fueled lynch mobs:** WhatsApp/Telegram coordination of real-time ethnic killings (India 2018, Ethiopia 2020–21) |
| **(b) Causing serious bodily or mental harm to members of the group** | **Holocaust:** Medical experiments (Mengele), starvation in ghettos, psychological torture; **Cambodian Genocide:** Torture at S-21 prison (electrocution, waterboarding), forced confessions; **Armenian Genocide:** Rape as weapon, forced marches with starvation; **Bosnian Genocide:** Systematic rape camps, ethnic cleansing trauma | **Cyber-torture via IoT:** Smart home systems weaponized for sleep deprivation, temperature extremes, sonic harassment (documented in domestic abuse contexts, scalable to group targeting); **AI-driven psychological warfare:** Customized disinformation campaigns targeting mental health (e.g., coordinated inauthentic behavior amplifying trauma narratives); **Neurotechnology abuse:** Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) for coercive interrogation (emerging military research); **Mass surveillance-induced trauma:** Predictive policing algorithms creating ambient terror (Uyghur communities via facial recognition grid, estimated 500,000+ surveillance cameras in Xinjiang) |

| **(e) Forcibly transferring children of the group to another group** | **Holocaust:** Kindertransport (some cases of forced separation); **Indigenous boarding schools:** Canada (150,000+ children), Australia (Stolen Generations), US (Indian boarding schools—350+ schools, 1860s–1970s); **Armenian Genocide:** Orphanages where children were forcibly Turkified; **Bosnian Genocide:** Forced displacement of children with families killed | **AI-driven child separation algorithms:** Predictive analytics flagging "at-risk" children from minority families for removal (documented bias in US child welfare algorithms disproportionately targeting Black and Indigenous families); **Deepfake parent impersonation:** Synthetic media used to facilitate abduction or justify removal; **Digital identity erasure:** Children of persecuted groups denied birth registration via corrupted databases (Rohingya children in Myanmar/Bangladesh border regions); **VR/AR indoctrination camps:** Immersive technology "re-education" for separated children (theoretical but technically feasible given VR torture documentation); **Social media grooming:** Algorithmic radicalization/recruitment of displaced children (ISIS documented use of Telegram for child soldier recruitment) |

### 4.1 Cross-Cutting Technology Themes

| Theme | Description | Forensic Implication |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Automation of Intent** | AI encodes discriminatory intent into "neutral" systems (credit scores, child welfare algorithms), making genocidal conditions appear as bureaucratic outcomes | Requires auditing algorithms for disparate impact on protected groups |
| **Ubiquity of Surveillance** | Smart city grids make oppression invisible and normalized—no visible ghetto walls required | IoT/sensor data becomes critical evidence of systematic conditions |
| **Platform Accountability Gap** | Encrypted platforms and recommendation algorithms diffuse responsibility | Need for corporate liability frameworks beyond individual speech prosecution |
| **Forensic Inversion** | Same technologies enabling atrocities create unprecedented evidence trails | Satellite imagery, metadata, sensor logs provide objective documentation |
| **Scalability Without Visibility** | Digital systems enable mass harm without physical presence of perpetrators | Jurisdictional challenges for prosecution; evidence may be in cloud servers across borders |

## 5. Organizations Directly Connected to Genocides

### 5.1 Perpetrator Organizations

#### The Khmer Rouge and Santebal (Cambodia)
The Khmer Rouge, the armed wing of the Communist Party of Kampuchea led by Pol Pot, governed Democratic Kampuchea (1975–1979). They utilized a brutal internal security police known as the **Santebal** to run torture and extermination centers, most notably the S-21 (Tuol Sleng) prison in Phnom Penh, where an estimated 17,000–20,000 prisoners were tortured and executed. Only seven prisoners are known to have survived.

**Post-Genocide Accountability:** The ECCC convicted Nuon Chea (Brother Number 2) and Khieu Samphan (Head of State) of genocide, crimes against humanity, and grave breaches of the Geneva Conventions.

#### The Interahamwe (Rwanda)
Formed around 1990 as the youth wing of Rwanda's ruling MRND party, the Interahamwe became the primary paramilitary perpetrator of the 1994 genocide. The name translates to "those who attack together" or "those who fight together" in Kinyarwanda. They established roadblocks, conducted house-to-house searches, and organized mass killings.

**Post-Genocide Accountability:** ICTR convicted multiple Interahamwe leaders. The organization persists as a rebel group in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo.

#### RTLM – Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines (Rwanda)
Nicknamed "Radio Genocide," RTLM was founded by Hutu hardliners and broadcast from July 1993 to July 1994. The station played a decisive role in inciting violence by:
- Disseminating anti-Tutsi hate propaganda
- Dehumanizing Tutsis as "cockroaches" (*inkotanyi*)
- Broadcasting names and locations of targets
- Directing militias to roadblocks and victim locations

**Landmark Legal Precedent:** The ICTR's *Prosecutor v. Nahimana et al.* (Media Trial, 2003) convicted RTLM founder Ferdinand Nahimana, director Jean-Bosco Barayagwiza, and editor Hassan Ngeze of direct and public incitement to commit genocide, persecution, and crimes against humanity. This established that media executives can be held criminally liable for broadcasts that incite genocide.

### 5.2 Modern Technology-Enabled Perpetrator Models

| Historical Model | Modern Equivalent | Accountability Gap |
|-----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| RTLM radio broadcasts | Facebook algorithmic amplification (Myanmar 2016–17) | Section 230-style immunity; algorithmic opacity |
| Interahamwe roadblocks | WhatsApp lynch mobs (India, Brazil) | End-to-end encryption; no broadcaster liability |
| Santebal secret police | AI predictive policing systems | "Neutral" algorithmic output; no identifiable human perpetrator |
| Khmer Rouge agrarian collectivization | Smart city resource denial grids | Infrastructure-as-policy; corporate-state partnerships |

## 6. Organizations for Justice, Prevention, and Research

### 6.1 International Tribunals and Legal Bodies

#### International Criminal Court (ICC)
Established by the 1998 Rome Statute, the ICC is a permanent court with jurisdiction over genocide, crimes against humanity, war crimes, and aggression. As of 2026, 125 states are parties. The Court operates on the principle of complementarity—it acts only when national courts are unwilling or unable to prosecute.

**Key Genocide Cases:** Darfur (Omar al-Bashir), Kenya (Ruto, Sang—charges withdrawn), Libya (Saif al-Islam Gaddafi), Mali (Ahmad al-Faqi al-Mahdi—destruction of cultural heritage), Myanmar (Rohingya—authorized investigation).

#### International Court of Justice (ICJ)
The principal judicial organ of the UN, the ICJ handles disputes between states and issues advisory opinions. It has jurisdiction over Genocide Convention disputes under Article IX.

**Key Genocide Cases:** *Bosnia and Herzegovina v. Serbia and Montenegro* (2007—found Serbia failed to prevent genocide but did not commit it); *Croatia v. Serbia* (2015); *The Gambia v. Myanmar* (2019–present—Rohingya); *South Africa v. Israel* (2023–present—Gaza).

#### Ad-Hoc UN Tribunals

**International Criminal Tribunal for the Former Yugoslavia (ICTY)**
Established by UN Security Council Resolution 827 (1993). Prosecuted 161 individuals. Key genocide convictions: Radislav Krstić (Srebrenica), Radovan Karadžić, Ratko Mladić. The Tribunal formally closed in December 2017, with residual functions transferred to the International Residual Mechanism for Criminal Tribunals (IRMCT).

**International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda (ICTR)**
Established by UN Security Council Resolution 955 (1994). Prosecuted 93 individuals. Landmark conviction of RTLM executives for media incitement to genocide. Closed in December 2015.

#### Extraordinary Chambers in the Courts of Cambodia (ECCC)
Established by the Cambodian government with UN assistance (2006) to try senior Khmer Rouge leaders. Hybrid court using both Cambodian and international law. Convicted Nuon Chea and Khieu Samphan (2014, 2018). The ECCC concluded its mandate in 2022.

### 6.2 Research, Education, and Prevention Organizations

#### International Association of Genocide Scholars (IAGS)
Founded in 1994, the IAGS is the largest international organization of scholars dedicated to research on the nature, causes, consequences, and prevention of genocide. Publishes the journal *Genocide Studies and Prevention*.

#### United States Holocaust Memorial Museum (USHMM)
Beyond its memorial function, the USHMM operates the **Simon-Skjodt Center for the Prevention of Genocide**, which conducts policy research, issues early warnings, and maintains the **Early Warning Project** (with the Dickey Center at Dartmouth)—a public statistical risk assessment for mass atrocities.

#### Aegis Trust
Campaigns against crimes against humanity and operates the Kigali Genocide Memorial in Rwanda. Works on education, documentation, and advocacy.

#### Yahad-In Unum
Founded by Father Patrick Desbois, this research organization investigates and documents mass executions of Jews and Roma in Eastern Europe during the Holocaust. Has located over 3,000 previously undiscovered mass graves.

#### Genocide Watch
Founded by Dr. Gregory Stanton (who developed the "Ten Stages of Genocide" framework). Monitors early warning signs and advocates for prevention.

#### The Sentinel Project
Uses technology (including machine learning) for atrocity prevention through early warning systems and community-based monitoring.

## 7. 21st Century Case Studies and Atrocity Crimes

### 7.1 Myanmar and the Rohingya (2017–Present)

**Context:** The Rohingya Muslim minority in Rakhine State has faced systematic discrimination since the 1982 Citizenship Law denied them citizenship. The Myanmar military (Tatmadaw) launched "clearance operations" in August 2017 following attacks by the Arakan Rohingya Salvation Army (ARSA).

**Atrocities Documented:**
- Mass killings (estimates: 9,000–13,000 killed in first month)
- Widespread rape and sexual violence
- Burning of 392 villages
- 740,000+ fled to Bangladesh

**Technology Dimensions:**
- **Facebook's role:** A 2018 UN Fact-Finding Mission found that Facebook played a "determining role" in the atrocities by allowing hate speech to spread unchecked. The platform was used to dehumanize Rohingya, spread disinformation, and coordinate violence.
- **Surveillance infrastructure:** Myanmar's military imported surveillance technology from Western and Chinese companies, including facial recognition and biometric data collection.
- **Satellite evidence:** Satellite imagery from Human Rights Watch and Amnesty International documented village destruction in real-time, providing crucial evidence for ICJ and ICC proceedings.

**Legal Proceedings:**
- ICJ: The Gambia v. Myanmar (2019)—provisional measures ordered (2020)
- ICC: Investigation authorized (2019) based on cross-border jurisdiction (Bangladesh is Rome Statute party)
- Universal jurisdiction: Argentina (2019), Germany (ongoing investigations)

### 7.2 Ethiopia and Tigray (2020–2022)

**Context:** Conflict erupted in November 2020 between the Ethiopian federal government (and Eritrean allies) and the Tigray People's Liberation Front (TPLF). The Tigray region was subjected to a communications blackout for over two years.

**Atrocities Documented:**
- Estimated 600,000 killed from violence, starvation, and denial of healthcare
- Widespread sexual violence (UN documented 1,300+ cases, likely undercount)
- Massacres in Axum, Mai Kadra, and other locations
- Deliberate starvation as weapon of war

**Technology Dimensions:**
- **Telecom shutdowns:** Ethiopia cut internet and phone services to Tigray for over two years, preventing documentation and humanitarian coordination.
- **Satellite evidence:** Commercial satellite imagery documented troop movements, village destruction, and agricultural burning.
- **Digital evidence:** When connectivity partially restored, citizen-documented evidence (photos, videos) provided crucial documentation.

### 7.3 Gaza (2023–Present)

**Context:** Following Hamas's attack on Israel on 7 October 2023 (1,200 killed, 250 taken hostage), Israel launched military operations in Gaza. As of early 2026, the death toll exceeds 50,000 according to Gaza health authorities, with widespread destruction of civilian infrastructure.

**Genocide Allegations:**
- South Africa brought a case to the ICJ alleging violations of the Genocide Convention (December 2023)
- The ICJ issued provisional measures (January, March, May 2024) ordering Israel to prevent genocidal acts and ensure humanitarian access
- ICC Prosecutor Karim Khan sought arrest warrants for Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, Defence Minister Yoav Gallant, and Hamas leaders (May 2024)

**Technology Dimensions:**
- **AI targeting:** Reports indicate Israel uses AI systems ("Lavender," "Where's Daddy?") for target identification and strike authorization, raising questions about human oversight and civilian protection.
- **Surveillance:** Gaza has been described as one of the most surveilled territories on earth, with extensive drone and sensor networks.
- **Information warfare:** Both sides have used social media extensively, with documented cases of AI-generated disinformation.
- **Digital documentation:** Despite telecom disruptions, citizen journalists and NGOs have used satellite phones and mesh networks to document atrocities.

## 8. Atrocity Prevention Architecture

### 8.1 Responsibility to Protect (R2P)

Adopted at the 2005 UN World Summit, R2P holds that:
1. States have the primary responsibility to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity
2. The international community should assist states in meeting this responsibility
3. The international community must take timely and decisive action through the UN when a state manifestly fails to protect its population

**Criticism:** R2P has been invoked unevenly (Libya 2011 vs. inaction on Syria, Myanmar) and has been criticized as a tool for Western interventionism.

### 8.2 UN Special Adviser on the Prevention of Genocide

Established in 2004, this office monitors situations worldwide, conducts early warning analysis, and advises the UN Secretary-General. The current Special Adviser (as of 2026) is Alice Wairimu Nderitu.

## 9. Digital Evidence, Corporate Accountability, and Prosecution

### 9.1 Digital Evidence in International Courts

International courts have increasingly relied on digital evidence:

- **ICC:** Uses satellite imagery, cell tower data, social media archives, and digital forensics. The ICC's Digital Forensics Team was established in 2019.
- **ICTY:** Pioneered use of satellite imagery (Srebrenica) and intercepted communications.
- **ECCC:** Used extensive photographic and documentary evidence from the Khmer Rouge era.

### 9.2 Corporate Accountability

#### Facebook/Meta and Myanmar
Following the 2018 UN report, Meta (then Facebook) commissioned the independent **Business for Social Responsibility (BSR)** human rights assessment, which found:
- Facebook's platform was used to spread hate speech and incitement
- The company's content moderation was inadequate for Myanmar's context
- The platform's design amplified divisive content

**Legal Actions:**
- Rohingya refugees filed a $150 billion class-action lawsuit in the US (2021)
- UK and EU legal actions pending
- Meta has not faced criminal liability for its role

#### Tech Company Liability Framework

| Jurisdiction | Framework | Applicability to Genocide Incitement |
|--------------|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| US | Section 230 CDA | Broad immunity for platform content; no genocide-specific liability |
| EU | Digital Services Act (2022) | Requires risk assessment for systemic harms; could apply to genocide incitement |
| UK | Online Safety Bill | Duty of care for illegal content; genocide incitement would qualify |
| Germany | NetzDG | Requires removal of illegal content within 24 hours; applies to hate speech |

### 9.3 The Accountability Gap

The RTLM precedent established that media executives can be held criminally liable for incitement. However, modern platforms present challenges:

1. **Algorithmic amplification:** Recommendation systems amplify content without human editorial decisions
2. **Encryption:** End-to-end encryption prevents content monitoring
3. **Scale:** Billions of posts per day make human review impossible
4. **Jurisdiction:** Tech companies are headquartered in the US; victims are in the Global South

**Emerging Legal Theories:**
- **Aiding and abetting liability:** Corporate knowledge of platform use for atrocities
- **Corporate complicity:** Failure to moderate despite known risks
- **Algorithmic intent:** Whether algorithmic design choices constitute intent

## 10. Irish and UK Legal Context

### 10.1 Ireland

#### Ratification and Implementation
- Ireland ratified the Genocide Convention on 22 June 1976
- The **Genocide Act 1973** (UK legislation, extended to Ireland pre-independence) was replaced by the **International Criminal Court Act 2006**, which incorporated Rome Statute crimes into Irish law

#### Universal Jurisdiction
Ireland has **universal jurisdiction** over genocide under customary international law and the ICC Act 2006. This means Irish courts can prosecute genocide regardless of where it was committed or the nationality of the perpetrator.

**Practical Barriers:**
- No genocide prosecutions have been brought in Irish courts
- Requires Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) consent
- Evidence collection from conflict zones is challenging
- Political will for universal jurisdiction prosecutions is limited

#### Relevant Irish Institutions
| Institution | Role |
|-------------|------|
| Irish Centre for Human Rights (NUI Galway) | Academic research, ICC monitoring |
| Front Line Defenders | Protection of human rights defenders at risk |
| Trócaire | Development and humanitarian work in conflict zones |
| Department of Foreign Affairs | Policy on R2P and atrocity prevention |

### 10.2 United Kingdom

#### Legal Framework
- **Genocide Act 1969:** Created the crime of genocide in UK law (now largely superseded)
- **International Criminal Court Act 2001:** Incorporated Rome Statute crimes, including genocide
- **Police, Crime, Sentencing and Courts Act 2022:** Includes provisions on genocide determination by UK courts

#### Universal Jurisdiction
The UK has universal jurisdiction over genocide. Notable cases:
- **Pinochet precedent (1999):** House of Lords ruled former heads of state lack immunity for torture (extended reasoning to genocide)
- **No genocide prosecutions to date** under universal jurisdiction

#### UK Institutions
